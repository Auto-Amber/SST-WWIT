What Is Steel Toe Teens?

Steel Toe Teens is a five-day summer camp run by Washington Women in Trades that gives girls ages 11–14 hands-on experience in the skilled trades. Campers rotate through real tools, real techniques, and real tradeswomen across nine different trades in a single week. Many of these girls have never held a wrench or touched a welder. Some will leave knowing exactly what they want to do with their lives. That's why you're here.

The camp runs July 20–24, 2026 at Shoreline Community College's automotive building. Your point of contact for anything that comes up during camp is Cynthia Payne (WWIT).

Daily Structure

Every day Monday–Thursday runs the same rhythm. Knowing this helps you pace yourself and know when transitions are coming:

9:00 – 11:45am Session 1: trade instruction at your station
12:00 – 12:30pm Lunch: campers eat together, you get a break
12:45 – 3:00pm Session 2: same trade, different group of campers

Sessions 1 and 2 bring different groups of campers to your station — so you'll run your content twice. Plan accordingly. The second run is almost always smoother than the first.

Your Contact
If anything comes up during camp — a behavioral situation, an incident, a camper concern, or anything you're unsure about — your point of contact is Cynthia Payne, WWIT. You will receive her direct contact information before camp begins. Do not handle serious situations alone.

Who Are the Campers?

Campers are girls ages 11–14, from a range of backgrounds across the greater Seattle area. Some signed themselves up because they're genuinely curious about trades. Others were enrolled by a parent or guardian. A few will arrive unsure why they're there.

Most have never been in a shop environment. They may not know basic safety etiquette, tool names, or how to follow technical instructions. That's expected and normal. You're not grading them — you're introducing them to a world they've likely never seen.

The modules that follow will give you specific tools for the situations that come up most often. Plan for about 30 minutes total to complete the training.

Understanding adolescent development & what to expect

The 11–14 Year Old Brain

mod1-intro
Module Introduction
Amber introduces the week, the camp, and what to expect from working with 11–14 year olds.
Video Coming Soon

The campers you'll work with are in one of the most intense developmental periods of their lives. Understanding a little about what's happening in their brains will make your week dramatically easier.

The teenage brain is under active construction. The prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for impulse control, judgment, and thinking through consequences — isn't fully developed until the mid-20s. This means teens aren't being dramatic when they seem to overreact. Their emotional responses are physiologically real and proportionally bigger than ours.

Key Insight
When a camper seems to be "making a big deal out of nothing," it is a big deal to her. Meet her where she is, not where you think she should be.

What Middle Schoolers Are Doing Right Now

At 11–14, girls are actively working on several developmental tasks simultaneously:

  • Identity formation: Figuring out who they are. Trying on personalities. Intensely aware of how they appear to peers.
  • Peer approval matters enormously: What a friend thinks can feel more important than what an adult thinks. Don't take this personally.
  • Testing limits: This is developmentally normal, not defiance for its own sake. They're learning where the lines are.
  • Short attention in unfamiliar contexts: A new environment with unfamiliar adults requires more energy. Expect more behavior management in the first hour of each day.
  • Huge variation: An 11-year-old and a 14-year-old are very different people. Some campers will act younger or older than their age. None of that is your problem to fix.

Your Role: Skilled Tradesperson First

You are here because you are excellent at your trade. That is your primary value to these campers. You don't need a teaching degree. You don't need to be a counselor. You need to show up as yourself, share what you know, and treat the campers with respect.

Your job is not to parent them, fix their problems, or be their friend. Your job is to give them a safe, hands-on experience in your trade for a few days. Everything in this training is about helping you do that more easily.

Remember This
You are a skilled professional sharing your expertise. That authenticity is exactly what makes Steel Toe Teens powerful. You don't need to be anything other than what you already are.
✓ Module 1 Check-In
1. A 13-year-old camper bursts into tears because her project isn't turning out how she envisioned. Your first response should be:
  • Tell her to toughen up — this is a trades camp, not art class.
  • Ignore it and let her work through it on her own.
  • Acknowledge her frustration calmly, then redirect toward a solution.
  • Call for a staff person immediately — this is above your pay grade.
2. Which best describes your role at Steel Toe Teens camp?
  • Surrogate parent for the week
  • Skilled tradesperson sharing your expertise in a supervised setting
  • Classroom teacher with behavior management authority
  • Peer mentor who acts like a cool older sister

Establishing expectations without militarizing the vibe

The First 15 Minutes Matter Most

mod2-tone
Setting the Tone
Watch a real example of how to open your station on day one—what to say, how to say it.
Video Coming Soon

How you open your station on day one sets the tone for the entire week. Campers are reading you — figuring out if you're approachable, if you know your stuff, and what the rules are. You don't need a formal speech. You need to be clear, calm, and confident.

A simple 2-minute intro covers everything: your name, what you do, what they're going to build, and the two or three non-negotiables for safety. That's it.

Safety Rules vs. Behavioral Expectations

Keep these in two separate categories in your mind — because they're handled differently.

  • Safety rules are non-negotiable and immediate. Eye protection, no running near tools, don't touch equipment until told. These are enforced every time, no exceptions, no discussions.
  • Behavioral expectations are about respect and engagement. Phones, side conversations, attitude. These have more flexibility and can often be redirected rather than confronted.
Pro Move
State your safety rules as facts, not threats. "Safety glasses stay on any time we're near tools — that's just the rule" lands better than "If you take off your safety glasses, you'll have to leave."

Authority Without Being Authoritarian

You've spent years earning respect on job sites. The dynamic with 12-year-olds is different, but the fundamentals are the same: competence, consistency, and fairness earn more respect than rank or volume.

✓ Works Well
"Let me show you how we do this on a real job site."

"I messed up a weld like that once. Here's what I learned."

Enforcing the same rule for every camper, every time.
✗ Avoid This
Singling out one camper for behavior others are doing too.

Raising your voice to establish authority.

Making it a power struggle in front of the group.

The Power of Noticing Positives

Tradespeople are trained to spot problems. At camp, deliberately shift some of that attention to what's going right. A specific, genuine compliment lands better than any correction.

"Nice work keeping that line straight" or "You asked a really good question just now" costs you nothing and builds enormous goodwill — especially with campers who are anxious or unsure.

✓ Module 2 Check-In
1. Two campers are talking to each other while you're explaining a technique. The best approach is:
  • Stop and call them out loudly in front of the group.
  • Pause, make brief eye contact, and continue — or redirect calmly without singling them out.
  • Ignore it so you don't create a scene.
  • Tell them they'll have to sit out if they keep talking.
2. A camper removes her safety glasses briefly "just to see better." You should:
  • Let it go this once — she'll put them back on.
  • Make it a teachable moment by explaining the physics of eye injury.
  • Immediately and calmly direct her to put them back on — every time, no exceptions.
  • Warn her that one more time and she leaves the station.

Working with campers who didn't choose to be there

When a Camper Doesn't Want to Be There

mod3-reluctant
The Reluctant Camper — Before & After
A scenario walkthrough showing what NOT to do vs. what actually works with a disengaged camper.
Video Coming Soon

Every year, some campers arrive because their parent signed them up — not because they asked to come. They may be sullen, disengaged, or even openly resistant. This is not a character flaw. It is a predictable response to being placed in an unfamiliar environment without full consent.

The worst thing you can do is make their resistance into a battle. The best thing you can do is give them a low-stakes reason to engage.

Reading the Signs

A reluctant camper often looks like:

  • Arms crossed, sitting back, minimal eye contact
  • Phone out whenever possible
  • Sarcastic comments or eye rolls at instructions
  • Slow or minimal participation in tasks
  • Doing just enough to not get in trouble

These behaviors are frustrating, but they are communication. She's telling you: "I don't trust this yet."

Important Distinction
Reluctance is different from disruption. A camper who is quietly disengaged but not interfering with others does not need to be "fixed" in the moment. Your goal is to make it easier for her to choose to participate — not to force it.

Strategies That Actually Work

  • Give her a task, not a lecture. Hands-on engagement often works when words don't. Ask her to hold something, pass you a tool, or just watch a step up close. Low-pressure entry points.
  • Ask questions that aren't yes/no. "What do you think happens if we overtighten this?" is more engaging than "Are you interested in welding?"
  • Don't take the resistance personally. Her attitude is about her situation, not about you. Staying warm and consistent in the face of coolness is one of the most powerful things you can do.
  • Look for the moment of genuine interest — and catch it. Almost every reluctant camper has a moment where something actually hooks her attention. When that happens, lean in gently, not dramatically.
  • Connect your trade to things she already cares about. Music venues, phones, gaming systems, clothing — skilled trades touch almost everything.
Real Scenario
A 12-year-old sits apart from the group, phone in hand, barely making eye contact. You say: "Hey, I don't care if you're not into this today — that's fine. But while you're here, you might as well see how this works. You don't have to do anything, just watch." Then you keep going. More often than not, she puts the phone down within five minutes.

When Reluctance Becomes Disruption

If a camper's disengagement starts affecting others — loud negative comments, convincing others not to participate, actively mocking the activity — that's a shift that needs a response.

Step one is always a quiet, private word: "Hey, I get that this might not be your thing. I need you to let the other campers enjoy it. Can you do that for me?" Most of the time, that's enough.

If the behavior continues, involve a WWIT staff member. That's not failure — that's exactly what they're there for.

✓ Module 3 Check-In
1. A camper with crossed arms and headphones in refuses to join the group activity. She's not bothering anyone. Your best move is:
  • Demand she remove her headphones and join immediately.
  • Give her a low-pressure entry point and keep going — she may join on her own terms.
  • Ignore her completely for the rest of the session.
  • Call her parent immediately.
2. A reluctant camper starts making sarcastic comments that are getting laughs and distracting the group. You should:
  • Publicly embarrass her to shut it down quickly.
  • Laugh it off — she's just being funny.
  • Speak to her quietly and privately, then involve a WWIT staff member if it continues.
  • Send her out of the station immediately.

In-the-moment techniques for tradespeople

Conflict Looks Different with Teens

mod4-deescalate
De-escalation in Action
Side-by-side scenario: escalating response vs. calm de-escalation. Spot the difference.
Video Coming Soon

On a job site, conflict is usually direct, fast, and resolved with words or a foreman. With 11–14 year olds, conflict is often interpersonal, emotionally charged, and involves bystanders who are very invested in what happens next.

Your goal in any conflict situation is always the same: lower the temperature, remove the audience where possible, and give everyone a way to move forward without losing face.

The De-escalation Sequence

  1. Lower your own volume and pace first. You regulate the room. If you stay calm, you give the camper something to match. If you escalate, you guarantee it gets worse.
  2. Create physical distance from the audience. "Can you come over here with me for a sec?" moves the conversation out of the spotlight.
  3. Acknowledge before redirecting. "I can see you're frustrated" costs you nothing and defuses faster than any logic. You don't have to agree — just acknowledge.
  4. Offer a path forward. "Let's take two minutes and come back to this" gives everyone an exit that isn't defeat.
  5. Don't demand the last word. If she mumbles something under her breath as she walks away, let it go. You won if the situation de-escalated. You don't need her to apologize to count it as a win.
Never Do This
Do not physically touch a camper to redirect or restrain, even if she seems out of control. Do not threaten consequences you can't enforce. Do not engage in a back-and-forth argument in front of the group. If a situation feels unsafe, remove yourself and other campers and call for WWIT staff immediately.

Redirecting vs. Confronting

Redirection means changing what's happening without making the behavior the centerpiece. It's a tool for low-to-medium situations that aren't safety issues.

✓ Redirect
"I need you on this side of the bench with me."

"Actually, let me show you this differently — come here."

"Hey, I've got a specific job for you right now."
✗ Confront (when avoidable)
"Why are you acting like this?"

"I've already told you three times."

"You're ruining this for everyone."

When to Involve Staff

You are not alone. WWIT staff are there to support you. Call for them — without hesitation — if:

  • A camper appears to be in emotional crisis (uncontrollable crying, shutting down completely)
  • There is physical aggression between campers
  • A camper discloses something concerning (abuse, self-harm, being in danger)
  • You feel unsafe or overwhelmed
  • A situation has de-escalated but you want it documented
✓ Module 4 Check-In
1. Two campers are in a heated argument near your station. Your first move is:
  • Get between them and loudly tell them to stop.
  • Stay calm, speak quietly, and try to separate them from the audience.
  • Let them work it out themselves.
  • Immediately send both to the office.
2. After you redirect a camper, she mutters something under her breath as she walks away. You should:
  • Call her back and address it immediately.
  • Make a formal report to WWIT staff about disrespect.
  • Let it go — de-escalation succeeded. You don't need the last word.
  • Make a pointed comment about it to the rest of the group.

Bias Incidents & Hate Symbols
How to respond when something serious happens

Why This Module Exists

mod5-incident
Bias Incident Protocol
Amber walks through exactly what to do and say if you encounter a hate symbol or bias incident at camp.
Video Coming Soon

In past camp years, volunteers have encountered situations where campers encountered or produced hate symbols and other targeted content. These situations require a specific, coordinated response — not improvisation. This module gives you the protocol and the language to use if something like this happens during your time at camp.

The First Rule
Do not handle a bias incident alone. Your job is to contain the immediate situation, protect the campers, and get WWIT staff involved immediately. The incident contact for Steel Toe Teens is Cynthia Payne. Know this name before camp begins.

What Counts as a Bias Incident

A bias incident is any act — written, verbal, drawn, or gestured — that targets a person or group based on race, ethnicity, religion, gender identity, sexual orientation, national origin, or disability. Examples include:

  • Slurs, spoken or written
  • Hate symbols (including swastikas, Confederate imagery, or other white supremacist symbols)
  • Targeted mocking or exclusion based on identity
  • Images, drawings, or graffiti with hateful content

You do not need to be certain of intent to respond. Impact matters regardless of intent. "I was just joking" does not change your response protocol.

Your Step-by-Step Response

  1. Stay calm and do not visibly react with shock or anger. Campers will take their cue from you. A calm, matter-of-fact response protects the other campers and avoids amplifying the incident.
  2. Immediately contact Cynthia Payne (WWIT). Do not wait to see how things develop. Make the call or find staff. This is always Step 2.
  3. Do not investigate on your own. Do not question campers, demand confessions, or try to determine who did what. That is a staff and organizational responsibility, not yours.
  4. If there is written/drawn content, do not erase or remove it until staff arrives, unless it is immediately causing distress. If you must remove it, tell staff what it said and where it was.
  5. Check on the campers who may have been directly affected. A quiet, calm "Are you okay? We're handling this" is enough. You don't need to counsel anyone. Just signal that it's being taken care of.
  6. Keep the rest of the group moving. Redirect to an activity. The worst outcome is a group of 12-year-olds standing around processing the incident without trained support.

What to Say to the Group

If campers ask what's happening or are visibly unsettled, a brief, honest, calm statement works:

Suggested Language
"Something came up that the camp staff is handling. It's being taken care of. Right now we're going to keep going with [activity]."

You do not need to explain, lecture, or discuss the incident with the group. That conversation belongs with trained staff, not in the moment with a group of middle schoolers.

What Not to Say

  • Do not say "It was probably just a joke" to minimize it for the group.
  • Do not say "Do you know who did this?" to the campers.
  • Do not make assumptions about a specific camper's guilt or innocence.
  • Do not express your own anger or frustration openly in front of campers, even if it's completely understandable.
Mandatory Reporting
If at any point a camper discloses abuse, neglect, or that she is in danger — regardless of context — you are required to report this information to WWIT staff immediately. You are not responsible for investigating. You are responsible for not keeping that information to yourself.
✓ Module 5 Check-In
1. You discover a swastika drawn in a bathroom. Your immediate next step is:
  • Erase it before the campers see it.
  • Ask the group if anyone knows who did this.
  • Leave it in place and immediately contact Cynthia Payne / WWIT staff.
  • Handle it yourself — address it directly with the full group.
2. A camper says something contained a racial slur "as a joke." You should:
  • Accept the explanation and move on.
  • Give a lecture on the history of the slur to the group.
  • Respond calmly, redirect the group, and report to WWIT staff regardless of intent.
  • Tell her it was wrong, give her a formal warning, and document it yourself.

Why You Being Here Is the Point

mod6-rolemodel
Why We Do This
Tradeswomen from Steel Toe Teens share what keeps them coming back as volunteers.
Video Coming Soon

For most of these campers, you may be the first skilled tradeswoman they've ever met in person. That is not an exaggeration. The statistics in construction, electrical, plumbing, and other skilled trades still show women under 5% of the workforce in many sectors.

They're watching how you carry yourself, how you handle problems, how you talk about your work. You don't have to give a speech about it. Just being confident and competent in front of them does the work.

Handling Their Questions

Campers will ask you things you might not expect. Some common ones and how to approach them:

  • "Do people treat you differently because you're a woman?"
    Be honest and age-appropriate. You don't have to share everything, but avoiding the question entirely sends its own message. Something like "Sometimes, yes. It's gotten better over time, and I've learned how to handle it" is enough.
  • "Did you always want to do this?"
    Share your actual story. Especially if it wasn't a straight line. Non-traditional paths are powerful for this age group to hear.
  • "How much money do you make?"
    Middle schoolers ask this. It's actually a great opportunity to talk about journeyman wages, apprenticeship, and the economics of the trades. Be as specific as you're comfortable being.
  • "Is this hard?"
    "Yes, and you can absolutely learn it" is the right answer in almost every case.

Your Own Imposter Syndrome Is Information

If you've ever felt like you didn't quite belong on the job site, or had to prove yourself twice to get half the credit — that experience is directly relevant to these girls. You don't need to process it with them. But knowing you've been where some of them are heading, and that you made it through, is quietly powerful.

What Success Looks Like

You won't be able to tell in real time whether you reached a particular camper. The payoff for this kind of work often shows up years later, when a girl remembers the electrician who showed her how to wire a switch, or the welder who told her she had a steady hand.

Success this week looks like:

  • Campers left with something they built with their own hands
  • At least one camper asked a follow-up question she wouldn't have asked on Monday
  • You stayed calm when something went sideways
  • You showed up as yourself
Thank You
Volunteering a week of your time to these campers is genuinely significant. The Women in Trades pipeline depends on people like you being willing to show up and share what you know. Steel Toe Teens exists because of volunteers. Thank you for being one.
✓ Module 6 Check-In
1. A camper asks "Do people treat you differently because you're a woman?" The best approach is:
  • Deflect — "That's not something we talk about at camp."
  • Answer honestly and age-appropriately from your own experience.
  • Tell her the trades are fully equal now and she has nothing to worry about.
  • Launch into a detailed account of every hardship you've faced.
2. A camper tells you this is "boring" and she's "bad at this." The best response is:
  • "Not everyone is cut out for the trades — that's okay."
  • Ignore the comment and keep going.
  • Acknowledge her frustration and affirm that learning something new always feels hard at first.
  • Challenge her to prove she's not bad at it by giving her the hardest task.
